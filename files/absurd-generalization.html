Absurd Generalization

On the mental shortcut that turns one thing into everything

Behaviour 30 Mar 2026

Someone lets you down once and becomes someone who always lets you down. A member of a group behaves badly and the group acquires the behavior as a property. You fail at something and the failure becomes a fact about your character rather than an outcome of specific circumstances. One data point, extracted from its context, scaled up to cover everything — this is the generalization, and it is one of the most common and most destructive cognitive moves available to the human mind.

We call it a logical fallacy when it appears in formal argument, give it a Latin name, and move on. What we do not do, with anything like sufficient regularity, is examine why it feels so compelling — why the single instance so readily becomes the universal rule, why the exception so easily becomes the category. The mechanism is worth understanding. Because it is not a mistake in the way that arithmetic errors are mistakes. It is a feature that has gone wrong. And features that have gone wrong are harder to correct than errors, because they were doing something useful before they started causing damage.

01   Why the mind generalizes

Generalization is not inherently a failure. It is one of the mind's primary tools for navigating a world that produces more information than any individual can fully process. You cannot evaluate every situation from first principles every time. You need patterns. You need categories. You need the ability to take what you learned in one context and apply it quickly in a new context that resembles the old one.

Touch a hot surface and learn to avoid that class of surface in future. Encounter someone who behaves aggressively and update your model of what aggressive behavior predicts. Experience an environment as unsafe and carry that assessment forward until given reason to revise it. These are not failures of reasoning. They are the architecture of learning. The brain is a prediction machine, and generalization is how it builds the models that make prediction possible.

The problem is calibration. A well-calibrated generalization is proportional to its evidence base — it covers the cases it has actually seen and remains genuinely open to the cases it has not. A poorly calibrated generalization leaps from the narrow sample to the universal claim, treating a few instances as definitive proof of a rule that applies everywhere and always. This is where the feature becomes a bug. Not in the generalizing itself, but in the confidence with which the generalization is held relative to the amount of evidence that supports it.

What makes calibration difficult is that the emotional weight of an experience and its evidential weight are not the same thing, and the mind tends to treat them as though they are. A single intensely negative experience with a person, a group, or a situation carries enormous emotional charge. That charge feels like significance. It feels like the kind of thing that should update your model substantially. And so it does — not because the evidence warrants the update, but because the feeling does. The generalization that follows is not proportional to the data. It is proportional to the pain.

02   The always and the never

The signature of an absurd generalization is the absolute. Always. Never. Every time. Not once. All of them. None of them. These words transform a particular observation into a universal law, and they do it so smoothly that the transformation often goes unnoticed — both by the person making the claim and by the people receiving it.

"You always do this." "They never take responsibility." "Every time I trust someone, this happens." "No one actually cares." These statements present themselves as conclusions drawn from repeated observation. They are almost never that. They are conclusions drawn from a subset of observation — usually the most painful subset — and extended to cover the entire class. The instances that don't fit the generalization are not absent from experience. They are absent from the generalization, which is a different thing.

The absolutism serves a function. It provides closure. If the person always does this, then what happened is not a complex situation requiring nuanced response — it is a simple situation confirming a known fact. If no one ever really cares, then the specific disappointment does not need to be processed in its particularity — it is just another instance of a pattern that was already understood. The generalization converts the ambiguous and painful into the certain and legible. The cost is accuracy. The benefit is not having to sit with the full weight of what actually happened.

This is also why absurd generalizations are so resistant to counter-evidence. If someone has decided that a person always lets them down, examples of that person not letting them down will not easily revise the generalization. They will be accommodated within it — as exceptions, as strategic behavior, as something that doesn't count for some reason. The generalization came from pain, and pain is not dissolved by a few pleasant examples. The mechanism that would revise a factual belief — encountering contrary evidence — does not work the same way on a belief that is doing emotional management as much as it is describing reality.

03   Generalization about people

The most damaging generalizations are the ones about people — specific people, and categories of people. They are the most damaging because they are the most self-reinforcing. When you have decided what someone is like, you interact with them through that decision, which shapes what you see, which confirms the decision, which shapes the next interaction.

The person who has been generalized as unreliable will find that their reliable behavior goes unremarked and their unreliable behavior is noticed and catalogued. Not through bad faith — through the normal operation of selective attention, which is directed by expectation. You see what you are looking for, not because you are dishonest but because attention is finite and the mind allocates it according to what it has already decided matters. The generalization becomes a perceptual filter, and the filter produces the evidence that maintains the generalization. This is a closed loop, and it is very hard to exit.

Generalizations about categories of people — groups defined by race, religion, gender, nationality, class — operate by the same mechanism at larger scale. A behavior observed in some members of a group becomes attributed to the group as a property, which means it is expected in all members, which means it is seen in all members even when it is not there, which means the generalization is perpetually confirmed. The individuals within the category cease to be individuals. They become instances of the category — evidence for or against a generalization that was already settled before they appeared.

What is striking about this, when you examine it closely, is how little it has to do with the actual properties of the people being generalized about. The generalization is primarily a statement about the mind doing the generalizing — about what it noticed, what it remembered, what it needed to be true, what it was unable to tolerate remaining uncertain. The other people are almost incidental. They are the raw material from which a conclusion was manufactured that the mind was already looking for a reason to reach.

04   Generalizing about yourself

The generalizations people make about themselves tend to receive less attention than the ones they make about others, but they are often more consequential. They are also often more absurd in the strict sense — more obviously unsupported by the evidence — and yet they are held with peculiar tenacity, resistant to counter-example in a way that generalizations about others sometimes are not.

"I am not the kind of person who can do that." "I always sabotage things when they start going well." "I have never been able to maintain anything." These are generalizations from a limited sample of experience, presented as facts about the self. They are treated as descriptive when they are often partly prescriptive — not just recording what has happened but shaping what will happen by establishing what is possible and what is not. The person who has decided they always give up will tend to give up, which confirms the generalization, which makes the next giving-up slightly more inevitable.

Negative self-generalizations are particularly sticky because they tend to arrive with a feeling of honesty. Holding a low view of yourself can feel like clear-eyed realism — not indulging in the delusion that you are better than you are, facing the truth, being appropriately humble. But a generalization is not honest simply because it is negative. It is honest if it is accurate, and accurate means proportional to the evidence. "I failed at this specific thing under these specific conditions" is accurate. "I am the kind of person who fails at things" is a leap — a generalization from the particular to the universal that the specific failure does not support.

The mechanism that produces negative self-generalizations is often the same one that produces negative generalizations about others: pain, looking for somewhere to land. When something goes wrong, the mind reaches for an explanation, and a general fact about the self is a stable explanation — it doesn't require re-examining each time. "I failed because I am the kind of person who fails" is cognitively efficient. It is also a way of punishing the self that presents itself as insight, which makes it harder to challenge than ordinary self-criticism.

05   The specific and the particular

The corrective to absurd generalization is not positive thinking, and it is not the injunction to be nicer in your assessments. It is specificity. The discipline of staying with the particular — with this person, in this situation, on this occasion — rather than allowing it to become a statement about all people, all situations, all occasions.

Specificity is harder than it sounds. It requires sitting with ambiguity rather than resolving it into a general rule. It requires maintaining the complexity of the individual case rather than filing it under a category. It requires something like patience with uncertainty — the willingness to not know what a pattern means yet, or whether there is a pattern, rather than reaching for the generalization that makes the not-knowing bearable.

It also requires noticing when you are generalizing and asking what the generalization is doing. What does it resolve for you? What does it make unnecessary to look at? What specific thing that happened is being converted into a general truth, and what is gained by that conversion? Not every generalization is covering for something. Some are genuine conclusions from genuine patterns. But the ones that are absolute, the ones that arrived suddenly after something painful, the ones that are strangely resistant to counter-evidence — those are worth examining.

The person who always lets you down probably doesn't. The group that never takes responsibility probably sometimes does. The self that always sabotages probably has also sometimes held on. The always and the never are almost always wrong. Not because the pattern isn't real — sometimes it is — but because always and never are not what you observed. They are what you needed to conclude. And what you needed to conclude is not the same thing as what is true.

The specific thing that happened is enough to work with. It does not need to be the proof of a universal law. It happened, and it meant something, and you are allowed to respond to it as what it was — a particular event, between particular people, under particular conditions — without drafting it into service as evidence for a conclusion about everything. That would be more accurate. It would also be harder. The generalization was a shortcut. The particular is where the actual work is.
