Some Claims Don't Require Evidence

Why this is wrong, and why it matters that it's wrong

Philosophy 12 Mar 2026

There is a family of positions that share a common feature: they begin by carving out a category of claim that is supposed to be exempt from the ordinary demand for justification. The claims in this category do not need evidence because they are self-evident, or because they are matters of faith, or because they are foundational — the kind of thing you have to accept before inquiry can begin. The move is ancient and it takes many forms, but its structure is always the same: here is a claim, and here is a reason why you may not ask me to defend it. This essay is about why that move fails, not in some exotic philosophical edge case, but in every ordinary instance where it is deployed. Evidence is not an optional feature of rational belief. It is the thing rational belief is made of, and the exemption categories, when you look at them carefully, do not hold.

01   The exemption is always self-serving

The first thing to notice about the claim that some claims don't require evidence is who tends to make it, and about which claims. It is not a general epistemological principle that people apply uniformly across the landscape of contested assertions. It is a selective exemption, invoked specifically for the claims the speaker already holds, the claims they arrived at before the argument started, the claims whose defense would be difficult or whose examination would be uncomfortable. The logic of the exemption is applied to the home team. Competing claims — the ones held by the other side — are still expected to clear the evidential bar. The asymmetry is so consistent that it is almost diagnostic. When someone tells you that a particular kind of claim doesn't require evidence, the question worth asking immediately is: would they accept that standard if their opponent applied it to a competing claim? The answer is almost always no, which tells you that the principle is not doing the work. The prior commitment is doing the work. The principle is what the prior commitment looks like when it has been asked to justify itself.

02   Self-evidence is not evidence

The oldest exemption category is self-evidence. Some propositions, on this view, are just obviously true — they are the kind of thing any rational person who understands the terms will immediately recognize as correct, without needing anything further. The proposition that two plus two equals four is given as an example. The proposition that cruelty is wrong is given as another. And from these reasonable-sounding examples the category expands, quietly, to include whatever the person doing the expanding would prefer not to defend. The problem is that self-evidence is not a property of the proposition. It is a property of the relationship between the proposition and a particular background of concepts, intuitions, and training. What seems self-evident to one person, in one cultural context, with one set of prior commitments, seems anything but self-evident to someone with a different background — and history is a long record of things that seemed self-evident to essentially everyone and turned out to be wrong. The intuition of self-evidence is real. It is a psychological event. But psychological events are not logical events, and the fact that a proposition feels as though it requires no defense is not the same as it requiring no defense.

This matters because self-evidence has historically been invoked to protect precisely the claims most in need of examination. It was self-evident that certain people were naturally suited to servitude. It was self-evident that the earth did not move. It was self-evident that the categories into which people were sorted were natural rather than constructed. In each case the self-evidence was doing the work that evidence was supposed to do — it was providing the sense of justification without the substance of it. When claims that had been held as self-evident were finally subjected to actual scrutiny, they did not hold. The lesson is not subtle.

03   Faith is a description, not a justification

The most common exemption in practice is faith. Certain claims — typically religious ones, though the category bleeds into others — are said to be matters of faith, meaning that they are held without evidence and that this is appropriate, even admirable. Faith, on this account, is a different mode of knowing, one that operates independently of the evidential standards that govern ordinary claims and that gives access to truths those standards cannot reach. This is a position with a long philosophical pedigree, and it deserves to be taken seriously rather than dismissed. But taking it seriously means asking what it actually says, and what it actually says is that the beliefs in question are held without evidence and that this is acceptable. It does not say that the beliefs are true. It does not say that they have been justified by a different kind of evidence. It says: I hold these beliefs, and I am choosing not to apply the standard that would otherwise determine whether holding them is rational.

That is a coherent choice. People make it all the time. But it is not a justification for the beliefs — it is a description of how they are held. Calling a belief a matter of faith tells you about the epistemic posture of the person holding it, not about the truth of the belief itself. And crucially, "this is a matter of faith" cannot function as a shield against the question of whether the belief is true, because the question of whether a belief is true is independent of the question of whether the holder considers evidence relevant. The world does not adjust itself to the epistemic standards of the people making claims about it.

04   Foundational claims need foundations too

A more sophisticated exemption is the foundationalist one. Every system of reasoning, on this view, has to start somewhere. If every claim requires evidence, and evidence is itself a claim, then you get an infinite regress — each justification requiring its own justification, forever. To stop the regress, you need foundational claims: basic beliefs that are not themselves justified by further evidence but that serve as the bedrock on which everything else is built. Logical axioms are given as examples. The reliability of sense perception is given as another. And from these structural necessities the category is again expanded to include whatever the person wants it to include.

The problem is not with the observation that some starting points are necessary. That is true. The problem is with what follows from it. The necessity of having starting points does not mean that any particular starting point is legitimate. It does not mean that starting points are immune to examination — they can be examined by their coherence, by their parsimony, by the work they do and the problems they generate, by whether they produce systems that match observation. And it does not mean that religious or metaphysical claims qualify as foundational in the relevant sense just because they are held with the same certainty as logical axioms. The axioms of logic are foundational not because someone decided they were exempt from scrutiny but because they are the conditions of possibility for any inference at all. That is a very specific and limited sense of "foundational," and it does not generalize to arbitrary claims about the nature of reality.

05   Intuition is data, not authority

There is a version of the exemption that appeals not to self-evidence or faith or foundations but to moral intuition specifically. Some things, it is said, we just know are wrong — the torture of innocents, the abuse of children, gratuitous cruelty. We do not need evidence for these moral facts because our intuitions about them are so strong and so universal that they constitute direct moral perception. The intuition is the evidence, or rather the intuition transcends the need for evidence, because no argument against these claims could be more compelling than the intuition itself.

This is a more defensible position than it might appear, and it is not crazy to take moral intuitions seriously as data. Strong, widely shared, stable intuitions are evidence — not conclusive evidence, but evidence — about the moral landscape. The mistake is in treating them as authority rather than data. Data can be examined, weighed, sometimes overridden by other data. Authority cannot. When intuitions are treated as authority, they are removed from the process by which we check whether they are tracking something real or whether they are tracking cultural conditioning, evolutionary pressure, or the particular distribution of power in the society that produced them. History offers extensive examples of strong, widely shared, stable moral intuitions that were wrong — about who counted as a person, about what kinds of treatment were acceptable, about the natural order of social arrangements. Intuitions as data remain in the conversation. Intuitions as authority foreclose it.

06   The standards we apply to others

There is a practical test for whether someone genuinely believes that some claims don't require evidence, or whether they are applying that principle only to their own claims. Present them with a claim from the opposing position and invoke the same exemption. Tell the religious believer that your atheism is a matter of faith and therefore beyond evidential challenge. Tell the person who holds certain moral claims as self-evident that your competing moral claims are equally self-evident and require no defense. Tell the foundationalist that your foundational commitments are as basic as theirs and equally immune to scrutiny. In almost every case, the response will be that no, this is different — that the exemption does not transfer, that the competing claim does require justification, that the standard applies asymmetrically.

This asymmetry is not a logical position. It is a tell. It reveals that the principle — some claims don't require evidence — is not actually functioning as a principle. It is functioning as a local defense, a moat around a specific set of beliefs, constructed after those beliefs were already in place. If the principle were genuine, it would apply symmetrically, and the person invoking it would be willing to grant the same exemption to competitors. The consistent refusal to do so is evidence that the person knows, at some level, that the exemption is not principled — that it is strategic, and that the standard they exempt themselves from is the standard they would use to challenge everyone else.

07   What evidence actually is

Part of the reason the exemption argument has traction is that "evidence" is often understood narrowly — as laboratory data, as empirical measurement, as the kind of thing that appears in scientific papers. On this narrow reading, the demand for evidence does look inappropriate for certain kinds of claims. Moral claims are not testable in a laboratory. Metaphysical claims about the nature of existence do not yield to controlled experiments. Mathematical truths are not established by observation. If evidence means only empirical measurement, then of course there are claims that don't require it — not because they are exempt from justification but because justification for them takes a different form.

But evidence, properly understood, is not limited to empirical measurement. It is anything that bears on the probability that a claim is true. Mathematical proofs are evidence for mathematical claims. Logical consistency is evidence for the coherence of a framework. Convergence across independent lines of inquiry is evidence for the conclusion they converge on. Historical record is evidence for claims about history. The strength and universality of a moral intuition is weak evidence for the claim it supports. None of this is nothing. All of it is subject to examination. Expanding the understanding of what evidence is dissolves most of the apparent exceptions, because the things that were supposed to be exempt from evidential requirement turn out to have their own forms of justification — forms that can be assessed, compared, and found more or less adequate. The exemption is not needed. What was needed was a broader understanding of what inquiry looks like.

08   The cost of the exemption

The argument for exemption feels protective. It feels like it is defending something valuable — religious belief, moral conviction, the starting points of reason itself — against a kind of aggressive rationalism that would demand justification for everything and be satisfied by nothing. There is a real concern buried in that feeling. Hyper-skepticism is a genuine pathology. A person who refuses to accept anything as adequately justified is not being epistemically virtuous — they are being epistemically paralyzed, which is a different thing. The demand for justification does not need to be infinite or unreasonable. It just needs to be consistent.

And consistency is exactly what the exemption forecloses. Once you accept that some claims don't require evidence — once you carve out a category that is beyond inquiry — you have no principled way to determine what belongs in the category and what doesn't. The category will be filled by whoever is doing the carving, and they will fill it with the things they want to protect, which are exactly the things most in need of examination. The exemption does not shelter important truths from unfair challenge. It shelters beliefs from the process by which they could be distinguished from important truths. That is not a small cost. It is the cost of not knowing whether what you hold is true, because you have decided in advance that the question cannot be asked. Everything that follows from a belief you have exempted from scrutiny is built on ground you have never actually tested. The structure may stand. But you will not know that it stands because it is solid. You will know only that you have not yet looked at the foundation, and that you have made a prior commitment not to.

The demand for evidence is not a weapon. It is not a tradition belonging to one side of any argument. It is the minimum condition for telling the difference between what is true and what is merely believed, and it applies — not as a punishment, but as a structural fact — to every claim that asserts something about the world. Exemptions from it do not protect claims. They protect claims from being known, which is a different thing, and not obviously a favor to anyone who cares whether what they believe is true.
