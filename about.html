What this is

People are strange. Not in the dramatic ways — not the headlines or the extremes — but in the ordinary, everyday ways that we rarely stop to look at directly. The habits we perform without knowing. The feelings we dress up as logic. The invisible agreements we hold with each other, and with ourselves, about how to be a person.

Most of this goes unexamined. Not because it isn't worth examining, but because it's too close. It's the water we swim in. The assumptions so foundational that pointing at them feels unnecessary, or strange, or vaguely rude.

That's what this site is for.

Marginalia are the notes people leave in the margins of books — reactions, questions, moments where a reader stops and says wait, that's worth thinking about. This is that, for everyday life. Observations on the small mechanics of being human: why we behave the way we do, what we're really after when we say certain things, what the ordinary patterns of social life reveal about something underneath.

The interest here is not in judging behaviour but in understanding it. Most of what people do makes sense once you find the right frame. The question is always: what does this actually serve? What need is being met, what fear is being managed, what story is being maintained? That question, applied patiently to ordinary things, tends to turn up something worth writing down.

The goal is not to be smarter about people. It's to be more honest about what's actually going on — including in ourselves.

These essays don't offer solutions. They don't end with five ways to improve. The ambition is narrower and, I think, more honest: to look at something familiar until it becomes strange, and then strange enough that you can actually see it. What you do with that is your own business.

Essays are published irregularly — when something has been observed long enough to have something real to say about it. There is no schedule. There is no newsletter. There is only the writing, when the writing is ready.

If something here finds you at the right moment, that's enough.
