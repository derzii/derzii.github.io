Why this exists

This started with frustration. The specific frustration of being misunderstood — not occasionally, not by strangers, but repeatedly, by people who were certain they had you figured out before you'd finished speaking. People who confused their interpretation of you with the truth of you. Who accused first and listened never.

At some point the frustration stopped being personal and became a question: why do people do this? Not just to me — to each other, to ideas, to anything that doesn't fit the story they've already decided on. What makes someone so certain? Where does that certainty come from, and what is it actually protecting?

Most of it, I think, comes from beliefs that were never chosen. Frameworks inherited so early they feel like facts. The kind of conviction that has never been seriously tested because it has always been surrounded by people who share it — which is not the same as being right. It just feels that way.

A belief you've never had to defend isn't really yours. It's just something that was handed to you and never taken back.

I'm not interested in telling people what to believe. I'm interested in the rarer thing: people who are genuinely willing to not know. Who can hold a question without rushing it to an answer. Who treat their own certainties as suspects rather than foundations. That kind of flexibility isn't weakness — it's the only honest response to how complicated people actually are.

These essays are an attempt to understand the underlying tendencies — the mechanics beneath the behaviour. Why we judge quickly and revise slowly. Why we perform conviction we don't entirely feel. Why we find people with different frameworks threatening rather than interesting. Why it's so much easier to accuse than to understand.

I'm not writing from a position of having figured it out. I'm writing because I haven't, and the not-knowing keeps producing questions worth following. The point isn't to arrive somewhere. The point is to look more carefully at what's already here.

If you've ever been flattened into a version of yourself you didn't recognise — reduced to someone else's assumption about you — you'll probably find something here that lands.
