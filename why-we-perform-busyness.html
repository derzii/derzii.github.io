Why We Perform Busyness

Observation June 2025

There is a particular kind of exhaustion that people wear proudly. Not the heavy, private kind that comes from grief or illness — but the performed kind, announced over coffee, bragged about in passing. "I've been absolutely slammed." "I haven't stopped all week." Said with the faint satisfaction of someone reporting a personal record.

We have built an entire social grammar around busyness. To be busy is to be needed. To be needed is to matter. And so we perform it — sometimes embellishing it, sometimes manufacturing it entirely — because to admit you have time feels uncomfortably close to admitting you are not important enough to have it taken from you.

The opposite of busyness isn't leisure. It's irrelevance. And that's what people are actually afraid of.

This is not a new observation. But what interests me is how thoroughly it has infiltrated the private self — how many people are not just performing busyness for others, but genuinely confusing motion for meaning in their own interior lives. Checking the phone again. Generating tasks. Filling silence before it can say anything.

There is a version of rest that has been made acceptable — the productive rest, the strategic pause. Meditation apps, "unplugging" weekends, structured journaling. Even recovery has been optimised. But the unstructured afternoon, the afternoon that goes nowhere and produces nothing — that remains quietly threatening.

What does it mean that we have to justify stillness? That rest requires a reason, a goal, a return on investment? Somewhere, the idea took hold that time only counts if it counts for something. And now we are all a little bit afraid of time that doesn't.
