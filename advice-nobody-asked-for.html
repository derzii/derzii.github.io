On Advice Nobody Asked For

Behaviour April 2025

Someone tells you about a problem they're having. Before they finish, you're already solving it. You offer the solution, they receive it politely, and you leave the conversation feeling helpful. They leave feeling vaguely worse, though they couldn't exactly say why.

Unsolicited advice is one of the more common ways we fail each other while believing we are helping. It masquerades as generosity. It is usually something else — a need to feel competent, to feel useful, to feel one step ahead. The advice isn't really for them. It's for us.

The urge to fix someone's problem before they've finished describing it is not empathy. It's impatience wearing a helpful face.

There's also something quietly presumptuous about unsolicited advice. To offer it is to assume the person hasn't already thought of what you're suggesting. It is to place yourself, automatically, in the position of someone with something to offer and them in the position of someone who needs it. This hierarchy is rarely stated. It is simply assumed, and felt.

What people usually want, when they share a problem, is not a solution. They want the problem witnessed. They want to feel that what they're carrying is real and heavy and understandable — not optimised away in thirty seconds.

The hardest thing to do in a conversation is to stay with someone in their difficulty rather than immediately reaching past it. To say "that sounds genuinely hard" and mean it, and stop there. Not because you have nothing to offer, but because the offering isn't what's needed yet.

Most of us were never taught this. We were taught that helping means doing something. But sometimes the most useful thing you can do is nothing except pay attention — completely, without an agenda, without the next sentence already forming. That is rarer than any advice. And it is almost always more valuable.
