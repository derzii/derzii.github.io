Science to Explain and Dumb Down Theology?

On what happens when certainty tries to explain certainty

Philosophy Apr 2025

There is a particular kind of argument that presents itself as rational but is almost entirely emotional. It goes like this: science has explained where we came from, how the brain works, why people feel what they feel, how the universe began. Therefore, religion is wrong. Therefore, God doesn't exist. Therefore, anyone who still believes is either ignorant, afraid, or hasn't thought hard enough.

This argument feels airtight to the people making it. It doesn't feel like an argument at all — it feels like a simple observation, like pointing at the sky and saying it's blue. And that feeling of obviousness, that sense that the conclusion is so self-evident it barely needs stating, is exactly what makes it worth examining.

Because the argument has a hidden assumption buried in it. A very large one. And almost nobody who makes it ever stops to look at what they're actually claiming.

01   What science actually does

Science is a method. Not a philosophy, not a worldview, not a answer to the question of meaning — a method. It is the most reliable method humans have ever developed for understanding how the physical world behaves. It works by forming hypotheses, designing experiments, collecting data, and revising conclusions based on evidence. When it's done well, it is self-correcting. It doesn't care what you want to be true. It follows the data.

This is extraordinary. It is, genuinely, one of the greatest achievements of human civilization. The fact that we can predict the behavior of subatomic particles, map the structure of DNA, describe the expansion of the universe from a single point 13.8 billion years ago — this is not small. It deserves the awe it receives.

But notice what science does and does not address. It describes mechanisms. It explains the how. How cells divide. How stars form. How neural patterns correlate with emotional states. It can tell you, with impressive precision, what happens and under what conditions. What it cannot tell you — what it is not designed to tell you — is what any of it means. Whether it matters. Whether there is a reason it exists at all rather than nothing.

These are not scientific questions. Not because science hasn't gotten to them yet, but because they are categorically different kinds of questions. The question "how did the universe begin?" is a scientific question. The question "why is there something rather than nothing?" is not. Science can describe the Big Bang in extraordinary detail. It cannot tell you why there was something there to bang in the first place, or whether the whole thing has a point.

The people who use science to dismiss theology are, most of the time, not actually engaging with theology at all. They are engaging with a caricature of it — a set of claims about the physical world that religion supposedly makes, which science then disproves. And yes, some of those claims exist. Young-earth creationism is not compatible with evolutionary biology. A literal six-day creation is not compatible with cosmology. These are real conflicts, and science wins them cleanly.

But this is not what theology is. Defeating young-earth creationism is not the same as defeating the question of whether existence has meaning. Explaining the evolutionary origins of religious belief is not the same as demonstrating that what religious belief points toward does not exist.

02   The category error

Imagine someone asks: "Is Beethoven's Ninth Symphony beautiful?" You could answer this question several ways. You could describe the acoustic properties of the sound waves produced when the symphony is performed. You could map the neurological activity in a listener's brain during the final movement. You could trace the historical and cultural context in which it was composed. You could analyze the mathematical relationships between the notes.

All of these would be accurate. None of them would answer the question.

The question of beauty is not a question about sound waves or neurons. It is a question about meaning, about value, about the relationship between a human being and an experience. Science can describe everything that happens when you hear that symphony. It cannot tell you whether it is beautiful, or whether beauty matters, or whether the fact that humans can be moved to tears by organized sound means anything at all about the nature of reality.

Theology, at its most serious, is asking questions of this kind. Not "did God make the world in six days" — that is a historical and cosmological question, and science is the right tool for it. But: is there a ground of being underlying existence? Is consciousness reducible to matter, or does it point toward something the physical sciences cannot fully capture? Is the human experience of transcendence — the near-universal sense, across every culture in history, that there is something beyond the purely material — evidence of something, or merely a useful evolutionary trick?

These are not easy questions. They do not have clean answers. But they are real questions, and dismissing them by pointing at the periodic table is a category error. It is bringing the wrong tool to the job and then claiming the job doesn't exist because your tool can't do it.

The philosopher David Chalmers calls the relationship between physical processes and subjective experience "the hard problem of consciousness." Why does any physical process give rise to an inner experience at all? Why is there something it is like to be you, rather than just a very complex information-processing system that behaves as if it has experiences? This is not a solved problem. It is not even close to a solved problem. And it sits right at the boundary between what science can address and what it cannot — which is exactly where the most interesting theological questions also sit.

03   What the dismissal is really doing

Here is what I think is actually happening when someone uses science to dismiss theology wholesale. They are not making an epistemological argument. They are performing an identity.

Being scientific, in the cultural sense, has become a tribal marker. It signals a particular kind of person: rational, evidence-based, not susceptible to superstition, not one of those people. And because religion has historically been associated with irrationality, with the suppression of inquiry, with the burning of heretics and the silencing of Galileo — rejecting it loudly is a way of placing yourself firmly on the right side of that history. Of being one of the good ones.

I understand the appeal. Religion has done real damage. It has been used to justify cruelty, to enforce conformity, to suppress women and minorities and anyone who asked the wrong questions. The history is long and ugly in parts, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest.

But there is a difference between having good reasons to be critical of religious institutions and their historical behavior, and claiming that science has demonstrated there is no God and the question is settled. The first is historically justified. The second is a non-sequitur dressed up as a conclusion.

The people making the second argument are often not the careful, rigorous thinkers they imagine themselves to be. They have simply replaced one set of unexamined certainties with another. They have traded inherited religious belief for inherited secular belief — absorbed from the culture around them, rarely interrogated, worn as identity. The mechanism is identical. Only the content has changed.

Real skepticism doesn't stop at religion. Real skepticism turns on everything, including the assumptions of scientific materialism itself. Including the belief that the physical is all there is. Including the confidence that consciousness is just neurons. Including the idea that meaning is a human projection onto a universe that has none. These are philosophical positions. They are not findings. They are assumptions that organize how you look at the findings.

04   The theology they are arguing against

Most people who use science to attack religion are attacking a specific version of religion — usually the version they encountered as children, or the most visible and loudest version in their cultural context. American evangelical Christianity. Literalist Islam. Orthodox Judaism in its most rigid form. Creationism in school boards. Homophobia justified by scripture. Priests abusing children while institutions protect them.

These are real targets and some of the anger is legitimate. But this is not all that theology is, and using these examples to dismiss the entire enterprise is like using the worst pop psychology to dismiss Freud, or using Soviet architecture to dismiss all architecture.

There is a tradition of serious theological thought that has engaged with exactly these questions for thousands of years. Augustine writing in the fourth century already understood that Genesis was not meant to be read as a geology textbook. Aquinas in the thirteenth century constructed philosophical arguments for God's existence that do not depend on any scientific claim being wrong. Kierkegaard in the nineteenth century was more rigorous in his self-examination than most contemporary atheists. Simone Weil, Dietrich Bonhoeffer, Paul Tillich, Karl Barth — these are not people who hadn't thought hard enough. These are people who thought harder than most of us ever will, about exactly the questions that science cannot answer.

You don't have to agree with them. I don't agree with all of them. But engaging with the best version of an argument, rather than the worst version, is the basic requirement of honest intellectual inquiry. Anything else is just winning arguments against people who aren't in the room.

05   The question underneath the question

Why does this matter? Why spend time on what is, at one level, just another internet-era culture war argument?

Because underneath the argument about science and religion is a question that almost nobody is actually asking, which is: how do human beings construct meaning, and what happens when that construction is disrupted?

Humans are meaning-making animals. This is not a metaphor. The drive to find pattern, narrative, purpose — to situate yourself inside a story that makes sense of suffering and death and love — is as fundamental to human psychology as hunger. You cannot remove it by deciding it is irrational. You can only replace it with something else.

The secular replacements are real and sometimes valuable. Progress as a narrative. Science as a guide to truth. Human rights as a moral framework. Community as a source of belonging. These work, up to a point. But they have limits that tend to become visible at the extremes — at death, at radical suffering, at the moments when the ordinary scaffolding of life collapses and you are left with the bare fact of existence and the question of whether it means anything.

Evolutionary biology can tell you that your grief when someone you love dies is a neurological process shaped by natural selection. This is true. It is also almost entirely useless when you are sitting with that grief. The explanation does not dissolve the experience. It does not answer the question the experience is asking.

Theology, at its best, is an attempt to answer that question. Not by denial — not by saying the suffering isn't real, or that everything happens for a reason in some cheap sense — but by situating the experience inside a framework large enough to hold it. Whether or not that framework corresponds to anything real is a genuine question. Whether the question is worth asking is not.

06   What honest engagement looks like

I am not making an argument for God. I am not suggesting you should believe anything in particular. I am not defending religious institutions or their histories.

I am making an argument for intellectual honesty, which includes honesty about what you know and what you don't know, and honesty about when you are arguing and when you are just performing a position.

And intellectual honesty requires acknowledging something uncomfortable: the confident atheist and the confident believer are, psychologically, doing the same thing. Both are closing off uncertainty. Both have found a framework that makes the world legible and are defending it rather than examining it. Both feel the warmth of being surrounded by people who agree with them. Both reach for their certainty fastest when they feel threatened. The content differs. The mechanism is identical.

The new atheist movement — Dawkins, Hitchens, Harris, Dennett — did something genuinely valuable: it pushed back against the idea that religious belief deserves automatic respect simply because it is religious. That it should be exempt from the scrutiny applied to other claims. That questioning it is somehow rude or taboo. This was a necessary correction, and it landed with real force at a moment when it was needed.

But in the process, it made a characteristic error of movements that are mostly right about one thing: it overextended. It went from "religious institutions should be held accountable" to "religion is a delusion." From "specific supernatural claims should be evaluated like any other claims" to "science has answered the deep questions." From healthy skepticism to a new kind of dogmatism that wears rationalism as its costume.

Richard Dawkins is a brilliant biologist. His work on evolutionary biology is important and deserves the influence it has had. But when he turns to theology, he engages almost exclusively with its weakest forms — a literalist God who intervenes in history, answers prayers, and competes with natural selection for explanatory power. He barely engages with the long tradition of apophatic theology, which holds that God is fundamentally beyond human concepts; or with process theology; or with the philosophical arguments from consciousness and contingency that don't depend on any particular reading of scripture. He refutes what he is already sure is wrong, rather than finding the strongest version of what he opposes. For someone who built a career on rigorous thinking, this is a striking failure of method.

07   The cost of cheap certainty

There is a price paid for settling this question too quickly, on either side, and the price is mostly invisible until you need what you gave away.

People who have used scientific rationalism to fully excise the religious impulse — not just institutional religion, not just bad theology, but the entire domain of transcendence, mystery, and the sacred — tend to discover, eventually, that they have made the world smaller than it is. Not in a supernatural sense. In a human sense. The experience of standing at the edge of the ocean at night, or holding a newborn, or sitting with someone who is dying — these experiences carry a weight that the language of neurons and evolution does not fully capture. Not because the neuroscience is wrong, but because the neuroscience is answering a different question than the one the experience is asking.

The question those experiences ask is not "what is happening in my brain right now?" The question is: does this matter? Is this significant? Is there something here that exceeds what I can measure and name? And the honest answer is that science has no response to that question, not because the question is primitive, but because the question is outside science's jurisdiction.

On the other side, the cost of cheap religious certainty is different but equally real. When faith becomes a fortress rather than a home — when its function is primarily to insulate you from doubt rather than to orient you toward what is true — it becomes brittle. It cannot survive contact with serious challenge. And the people whose faith was always primarily about comfort and identity, rather than genuine inquiry, are the ones who either shatter when challenged or become aggressive in defending what they've never truly examined. Both responses are symptoms of the same underlying problem: a relationship with certainty that never learned to coexist with not knowing.

The theologian Paul Tillich described faith not as the absence of doubt but as the courage to affirm meaning in the face of doubt. This is harder than either confident belief or confident disbelief. It requires living in the tension rather than resolving it cheaply. Most people find both easier alternatives preferable. But easier is not the same as better, and comfortable is not the same as true.

What would it look like to hold the question honestly? Probably something like this: to take the findings of science seriously and follow them wherever they lead, including when they challenge inherited religious assumptions. To take the questions of theology seriously and sit with them genuinely, rather than dismissing them as primitive or resolving them with inherited answers. To notice when you are genuinely inquiring and when you are defending a position you've already decided on. To be willing, at the limit, to say: I don't know. And to mean it — not as a temporary holding pattern until the right argument comes along, but as a genuine acknowledgment of where the boundary of human knowledge actually sits.

Most people never get there. The not-knowing is too uncomfortable, the certainty too warm. But the people who do get there — on both sides of the supposed divide — tend to find that the conversation becomes more interesting, not less. That the questions open up rather than close down. That there is more to think about, not less.

Science has not explained away theology. It has clarified which theological claims are empirically testable and found some of them wanting. That is valuable and important work. But the deep questions remain exactly where they were — unanswered, serious, worth your attention. Whether you approach them through the language of physics or the language of prayer or the language of philosophy, the questions are the same. And the honest answer to most of them is still: we don't know. We are working on it. We may never fully get there.

That is not a failure. That is what it looks like to be human, looking out at a universe that exceeds our understanding, and refusing to pretend otherwise.

If you think there is no God, that's a defensible position. Some of the best minds in history have held it. But hold it the way it deserves to be held — as a philosophical position, with acknowledged uncertainty, with awareness of what it doesn't resolve and what it leaves open. Not as a finding. Not as something science proved. Not as evidence that you are smarter than the people who disagree with you.

Because the people who disagree with you, the serious ones, are not confused about science. They are asking a different question. They are asking whether the universe that science describes so well has any meaning at all, whether consciousness is more than matter, whether the human experience of transcendence points toward something real. These questions do not have easy answers. They may not have answers at all. But they are not stupid questions, and treating them as stupid says more about you than about the questions.

The most honest position, the one that actually reflects the state of human knowledge, is something like this: science is extraordinarily powerful at describing physical reality, and we should follow it wherever it leads, including when it conflicts with religious claims about the physical world. At the same time, there are questions that science is not designed to answer — questions about meaning, consciousness, value, and the ground of being — and on these questions, genuine uncertainty is the only intellectually defensible stance.

That is a harder position to hold than either "God exists and the Bible is literally true" or "science has disproved religion and only fools believe." It requires sitting with ambiguity. It requires admitting that you don't know. It requires giving up the comfort of certainty in exchange for something more honest.

Most people find that trade too expensive. Which is why most people don't make it. Which is why the same argument keeps happening, on both sides, forever — each side equally certain, equally unwilling to look at what they're actually claiming, equally performing rather than thinking.

The question isn't whether science can dumb down theology. It can't, because it's not even asking the same questions. The question is whether you're willing to take the questions seriously — all of them, including the ones that don't have clean answers, including the ones that make you uncomfortable, including the ones that your tribe has already decided are settled.

Most people aren't. And that is the real problem — not science, not religion, but the human preference for the comfort of certainty over the discomfort of actually not knowing.
