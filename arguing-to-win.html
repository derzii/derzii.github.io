Arguing to Win, Not to Know

Philosophy February 2025

There are two different things a person can want from an argument. They can want to win it, or they can want to understand something better by the end of it. These goals are not compatible, and most arguments are lost — for everyone — because no one is honest about which one they're actually after.

Arguing to win is the default. It's what debate training teaches, what social media rewards, what most of us learned by watching the adults around us disagree. The goal is to remain standing at the end. Changing your mind is conceding. Acknowledging a good point is giving ground. The other person is an opponent, not a resource.

If you're only listening to find the flaw, you're not in a conversation. You're in a performance of one.

Arguing to know requires something most people find genuinely difficult: treating the other person's view as a possible correction to your own. Not a threat. Not something to be dismantled. A possible improvement on what you currently believe. This means listening differently — not for the weakness in their argument, but for whatever is true in it. Even if it's only partially true. Even if it means the picture gets more complicated.

The clearest sign someone is arguing to win rather than to know is how they respond to a good point from the other side. If the response is to immediately pivot, to find a new angle of attack, to keep pressing rather than pausing — they weren't really listening. They were waiting.

Changing your mind in public is rare because it looks like losing. But it's actually the most interesting thing that can happen in a conversation. It means something real occurred — not just an exchange of positions, but an actual event in someone's thinking. Those moments are worth more than any argument you could win.

The question worth asking before any disagreement: do I want to be right, or do I want to understand what's actually true? They're not always the same thing. And most of us, if we're honest, have a strong preference for one over the other.
