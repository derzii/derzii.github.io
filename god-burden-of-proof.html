You Can't Disprove It

On burden of proof, and the move that looks like an argument but isn't

Philosophy 19 Apr 2026

There is a move in certain arguments that looks like a defense but is actually a forfeit. It goes like this: you cannot prove that God does not exist, therefore God exists. Or more softly: you cannot disprove it, so belief is reasonable. This move appears frequently in conversations about religion, but it also appears in conversations about ghosts, about homeopathy, about conspiracy theories, about any claim whose proponents have noticed that absence of disproof feels like a kind of permission. It is worth taking seriously — not because it is a strong argument, but because it is a common one, and because understanding precisely why it fails illuminates something important about how claims are supposed to work.

01   What a claim is

When someone makes a claim — any claim — they are doing something specific. They are asserting that the world contains a feature it might not contain. They are drawing a distinction between a world with that feature and a world without it, and saying: we are in the first kind. This is not a neutral act. It is an act that requires justification, because the world is under no obligation to contain any particular thing, and the mere conceivability of a thing provides no evidence that the thing exists. Unicorns are conceivable. They are not thereby real. The conceivability of God — the fact that we can form the concept, that the concept is internally coherent enough to discuss — does not constitute evidence for God's existence. It constitutes evidence only that humans are capable of forming the concept, which is not a surprising fact about humans.

The burden of proof is the name for the obligation that attaches to the person making a claim. It is not a rhetorical convention or an arbitrary rule of debate. It reflects something structural about the relationship between claims and evidence. The person asserting a feature of the world is the person who has introduced that feature into the conversation, and therefore the person who must supply the grounds for thinking the feature is real. This is not a demand that believers alone must satisfy — it applies symmetrically to every positive claim. The atheist who claims God definitely does not exist carries the same burden as the theist who claims God definitely does. Both are asserting features of the world. Both need to defend those assertions.

02   The asymmetry of absence

The argument from non-disproof exploits a real asymmetry, but misidentifies its significance. It is true that it is very difficult — often impossible — to prove the non-existence of something. This difficulty is real, and it is not something the skeptic can simply dismiss. But the difficulty of disproving a claim does not transfer evidential weight to the claim. These are separate questions. How hard is it to disprove X? and How much evidence do we have for X? do not answer each other. They are orthogonal. The fact that I cannot prove there is no dragon in my attic does not provide any support for the proposition that there is a dragon in my attic. It just tells you something about the epistemic limitations around proving universal negatives — which is interesting, but not relevant to whether I should believe in the dragon.

The reason this matters is that the universe of things I cannot disprove is essentially infinite. I cannot disprove that the world was created last Thursday with false memories intact. I cannot disprove that there are seventeen invisible gods who have agreed not to interfere. I cannot disprove that gravity is maintained by the ongoing effort of beings who exist outside spacetime. All of these are, in a technical sense, unfalsifiable — and therefore all of them survive the non-disproof test. If surviving that test is sufficient for rational belief, then rational belief becomes indiscriminate, which is to say it ceases to be rational belief and becomes something else: an open door through which anything can walk.

The burden of proof is not a punishment for believers. It is the mechanism by which any claim — including this one — earns the right to be taken seriously.

This is what Bertrand Russell was pointing at with his teapot. There is, he suggested, a china teapot in orbit around the sun, too small for telescopes to detect. You cannot disprove it. Does that make belief in the teapot reasonable? The point is not to mock religious belief by comparing God to a teapot — the point is to isolate the logical structure of the argument and ask whether we would accept it in a case where we have no prior attachment to the conclusion. In that case, almost no one would. The non-disproof of the teapot provides no reason to believe in the teapot. Whatever work the argument does in the religious case, it is not being done by the logic.

03   What unfalsifiability costs

There is a version of the God claim that is explicitly constructed to be unfalsifiable. God is defined as existing outside space and time, as having no necessary relationship to observable events, as being a being whose presence or absence would look identical from the inside of a universe like ours. This is a philosophically sophisticated move — it insulates the claim from empirical refutation — but it comes at a cost that is not always acknowledged. A claim that makes no predictions about the observable world cannot be confirmed by the observable world. The insulation runs in both directions. If no possible observation would count as evidence against God's existence, then no possible observation counts as evidence for it either. The claim has been removed from the domain in which evidence operates, which means evidence cannot help it any more than hurt it.

This is not proof that God does not exist. It is a statement about the epistemological status of a certain kind of God claim. If the claim is constructed to be beyond evidence, then the appropriate response is not belief and not disbelief but suspension — a recognition that this particular claim has placed itself outside the territory where the tools of rational inquiry function. Some people find this liberating; it means faith is not subject to empirical challenge. Others find it troubling; it means faith is not subject to empirical support either. Both responses are tracking the same underlying fact, which is that a claim that cannot be tested cannot be confirmed. You cannot have the epistemic immunity without paying the epistemic price.

04   The default position

The non-disproof argument usually contains an implicit assumption that the two positions — belief and non-belief — are symmetrical, that without definitive proof in either direction we should treat them as equally reasonable, or perhaps even that belief is the prior and non-belief requires justification. This framing is worth examining. The question of what we should believe in the absence of evidence is the question of what the default position is, and the answer depends on how we think about claims in general.

If someone tells you that a coin is fair, you have some prior reason to believe them — coins are generally fair, the claim is ordinary, the prior probability is not negligible. If someone tells you that the coin is sentient, the prior is very different. The prior for the existence of a particular kind of entity — especially a maximally unusual entity, one that exists outside the categories of physical things entirely — is not fifty percent. It is not zero either, because we are genuinely uncertain. But it is not the kind of uncertainty in which both sides are equivalently supported in the absence of evidence. The default in the absence of evidence is not belief. The default is the question: what are we to make of entities we have no evidence for?

The answer that does the least violence to how rational inquiry works is: we neither assert their existence nor assert their non-existence; we assign them low prior probability and update on evidence. This is not atheism, exactly — it is closer to what is usually called agnosticism, though the word has been flattened by use. It is the recognition that absence of evidence is not evidence of absence, but that it is also not evidence of presence, and that the honest position when evidence is lacking is not to assign equal probability to every conceivable claim but to ask what we would reasonably expect to see if the claim were true and whether we see it.

05   What the argument is actually doing

When someone says "you can't disprove God, so He exists," they are usually not making a philosophical argument in good faith about epistemic standards. They are defending a position they already hold, against a challenge they did not expect to need to answer. The argument is a closing move, not an opening one. It is deployed when the expected deference to the claim has not materialized, and it functions to reframe the conversation so that the skeptic appears to be the one making an unsupported assertion. This is rhetorically effective because it is partially true — the strong atheist does bear a burden — but it is deployed asymmetrically, as though placing a burden on the skeptic removes the burden from the believer, which is not how burden of proof works. Both burdens exist simultaneously. Pointing at yours does not discharge mine.

There is a more honest version of the position that this argument is trying to reach. It would say something like: I believe in God not because I can demonstrate his existence through publicly available evidence, but because of experiences I have had, because of the explanatory work the concept does for me, because the framework of belief makes my life coherent in ways that non-belief does not. This is an honest account of why many people believe, and it does not pretend to be more than it is. It does not claim to obligate anyone else. It does not lean on the non-disproof argument because it does not need to — it is not trying to demonstrate that belief is epistemically required, only that it is personally motivated. That is a position that can be taken seriously, because it is honest about what it is doing.

The non-disproof argument is trying to do something more — to establish that belief is rationally mandated, or at least that skepticism is equally unsupported, and it fails at both. The failure is not a failure of religion. It is a failure of this particular argument, which borrows the structure of a logical defense while quietly setting aside the standard that would make the defense meaningful. Pointing that out is not an attack on faith. It is an insistence that arguments mean what they say, and that the tools we use to evaluate claims apply to all claims including this one, which is just a way of taking the question seriously enough to think about it carefully.

The burden of proof is not a rhetorical weapon. It is the minimum condition for intellectual honesty — the acknowledgment that the world does not automatically contain what we want it to contain, and that asserting it does requires us to do some work. That standard does not resolve the question of God's existence. It does not tell us what the universe is made of or what, if anything, explains why there is something rather than nothing. It just tells us which direction to face when we are looking for answers: toward evidence, toward reasons, toward arguments that do not quietly require us to abandon the standards by which we evaluate arguments. That is a small demand. Most questions worth asking can meet it.
