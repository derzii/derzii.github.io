The Internet Doesn't Deserve Your Sanity

On pseudo-profundity, attention crabs, and choosing what to ignore

Observation 19 Feb 2026

The internet was supposed to be the escape. Not in an idealistic sense — not the utopia of free information and connected humanity that people sold in the nineties. Just the practical escape. The place you go to not be wherever you are. To decompress. To exist for a while without the weight of the things you are choosing, for your own sanity, to not think about. That was the deal. It was a reasonable deal. And somewhere along the way, without anyone formally agreeing to this, the deal was broken. The place that was supposed to offer relief became another surface for the same culture it was supposed to provide relief from. Another room full of the same noise, just louder, faster, and with an algorithm deciding which noise you receive.

What replaced the escape was something specific, and it is worth naming precisely: a culture organized around belittlement, performed ethics, and the strategic use of vague ideas to sort people into groups that can then be set against each other. None of this is accidental. All of it serves someone. The question is whether it serves you — and the answer, almost certainly, is that it does not.

01   Pseudo-profundity

There is a particular kind of statement that circulates online with high velocity and low content. It presents itself as insight. It uses the register of wisdom — the compressed, aphoristic form that genuine observations sometimes take — but it is hollow at the center. It does not describe anything specific. It does not survive the first serious question. It exists primarily to be agreed with by one group and resented by another, which is its actual function. Not to illuminate. To sort.

"Don't just respect X, you have to respect all X." Statements of this form are the template. On the surface: a call for consistency, for fairness, for not applying standards selectively. Underneath: a strawman, pre-assembled. The implied accusation is that the person reading this has been withholding respect from some group they are now being instructed to include. The statement does not demonstrate this. It assumes it. And it smuggles in, without argument, the premise that respect is something owed — that certain categories of person, by virtue of belonging to those categories, are entitled to your regard.

This premise is worth examining rather than absorbing. No one owes respect for what was not chosen and cannot be evaluated. Respect in the meaningful sense — not basic human decency, which is different, but the specific elevated regard implied by these statements — is a response to something. It is earned or it is not. Declaring it a universal obligation does not make it one. It just makes the declaration feel virtuous to the people who agree with it and aggressive to the people who don't, which produces exactly the engagement the algorithm rewards.

The pseudo-profound statement works by being simultaneously too vague to refute and too charged to ignore. It collapses a complex question — when is respect owed, on what basis, toward what ends — into a slogan that pre-answers the question while appearing to raise it. The people who already agree treat it as self-evident. The people who disagree are implicitly accused of the failure the statement describes. Neither group has thought anything through. Both groups have been activated. That is the entire mechanism.

02   The performance of ethics

Everyone online is ethical. This is the most reliable observation you can make about the internet. Every person, in the presentation of themselves, is on the right side. Is standing for the correct things. Is appalled by what should be appalling and supportive of what should be supported. The ethical positioning is the first thing established and the most aggressively maintained.

What is interesting is how quickly this positioning collapses when the questions get specific. The person who is loudly committed to a principle tends to hold that commitment with great firmness right up until the principle conflicts with their pre-established beliefs — at which point the principle develops exceptions, or the situation turns out to be more complicated than it appeared, or the person simply stops engaging. The ethical performance was always downstream of the beliefs. The beliefs were there first. The ethics were the costume.

This is not hypocrisy in the ordinary sense. Most of these people are not consciously performing something they do not feel. They genuinely believe they are consistent. The inconsistency is invisible to them because the pre-established beliefs are operating as the perceptual filter through which everything, including their own ethical commitments, is evaluated. You cannot see your own filter. You can only see what it lets through, which always appears to confirm the filter's validity.

What this produces, at scale, is a culture in which ethical language has been almost entirely decoupled from ethical reasoning. The language is abundant. The reasoning is rare. And the abundance of the language makes the rarity of the reasoning harder to notice, because the surface looks exactly the same whether the substance is there or not.

03   What self-improvement is actually for

There is a version of self-improvement that is entirely about presentation. You go to the gym to be seen as someone who goes to the gym. You read to be seen as someone who reads. You adopt certain positions to be seen as someone who holds those positions. The improvement is real in a technical sense — the body changes, the information enters the mind — but its function is external. It is performed for an audience, which means its value is entirely dependent on the audience's response, which means the person doing it will never be satisfied by it, because no audience response is ever final or sufficient.

When you reach the body you were working toward and find that the dissatisfaction is still there, you have discovered something important: the dissatisfaction was never about the body. The body was the proposed solution to a problem the body was never going to solve. The actual problem — whatever it was — is still there, now dressed differently.

What you do for yourself is not a spectacle. This is not a motivational observation. It is a structural one. The things that actually change a person — the confrontation with a difficult idea, the honest examination of a belief you have been protecting, the slow and unglamorous work of becoming more honest about what you are and what you want — these do not photograph well. They do not produce content. They are not legible to an audience, and they were not designed to be. They were designed to be useful to the person doing them, which is a completely different standard. You are the only one who can assess whether they worked. No one else has access to the interior where the change is or is not happening.

The fixation on external validation for internal work is not just ineffective. It is a substitution. You are spending the energy that the actual work requires on something that produces the feeling of the work without the work itself. Which means the actual work does not get done. Which means the actual problem does not get addressed. The audience response fills the space where the change was supposed to go.

04   The algorithm is not your friend

The algorithm does not know you. It knows your behavior — what you clicked, how long you stayed, what made you come back. From this behavior it constructs a model of what will keep you engaged, and it feeds you that. The model is accurate enough to be effective and inaccurate enough to be harmful. It knows what activates you. It does not know what is good for you, and it does not care. Those are not the variables it optimizes for.

What the algorithm is very good at is identifying and amplifying the content that produces strong reactions — outrage, contempt, fear, the satisfying feeling of having your existing beliefs confirmed by new evidence. These reactions keep you on the platform. They are also, over time, corrosive. They narrow what you are willing to encounter. They make the familiar more familiar and the unfamiliar more threatening. They produce a version of you that is increasingly reactive, increasingly sorted into a position, increasingly unable to engage with anything that does not fit the model the algorithm has built from your previous engagement.

You did not choose this. Nobody chose it. It emerged from the optimization of engagement metrics by systems that have no stake in your cognitive health or your ability to think clearly about the world. The people who built these systems are not malicious in any interesting sense. They built something that worked by the measures they were measuring. The measures were wrong. The thing still works, by those measures. You are the externality.

The practical question is what to do about it. And the answer that requires the least abstraction is also the most honest: decide what you are willing to let have access to your attention, and protect that decision. Not because the things you are refusing are necessarily wrong. Because your attention is finite and its quality matters, and the things competing for it are not competing on the basis of whether they deserve it. They are competing on the basis of whether they can take it. Those are not the same thing. You are allowed to notice the difference.

05   The case for selective ignorance

Ignorance has a bad reputation. It is treated as a failure — of curiosity, of responsibility, of engagement with the world. The informed person is the virtuous person. You should know what is happening. You should care about it. You should have a view. The pressure to be informed about everything, to have processed the latest outrage and arrived at the correct response to it, is relentless and largely unexamined.

But there is a kind of ignorance that is not a failure. It is a choice about where to put a finite resource. Your attention, your emotional energy, your capacity for genuine engagement — these are not unlimited. They are spent on whatever you give them to, which means spending them on the algorithm's preferred inputs is not neutral. It is a decision about what you are not spending them on. Every hour of outrage consumption is an hour not spent on something that might actually change your relationship to your own life. This is not a small trade.

The things happening in the world — the distant conflicts, the political performance, the cycling outrage, the latest instance of something that has been happening since the beginning of civilization and will continue regardless of your awareness of its most recent manifestation — most of these things are not changed by your distress about them. Your distress is real. Its effect on the situation is negligible. What it does affect is you: your baseline anxiety, your ability to think clearly, your capacity to engage with the things that are actually within your reach.

Choosing not to be consumed by what you cannot affect is not apathy. It is resource management. It is the recognition that caring about everything is caring about nothing — that attention spread infinitely thin is not attention at all, and that the performance of awareness is not the same as the practice of it. You can be informed without being consumed. You can care without making the caring the central fact of your existence. You can look at what is happening and choose, deliberately, how much of yourself to give to it.

The algorithm does not deserve your sanity. The distant outrage does not deserve your peace. The pseudo-profound statement designed to activate you does not deserve the activation. These are not passive thieves. You are handing them what they take. The question is not whether bad things are happening — they are, they always are, they always will be. The question is whether your awareness of them, in the form the algorithm delivers it, is making you more capable of living well or less. Whether the engagement is clarifying something or just maintaining a level of agitation that keeps you coming back.

Evil eventually falls. Not because the arc of history bends toward justice in any guaranteed sense, but because things that exist only to consume eventually run out of what they are consuming. The cancerous thing collapses. The regime overextends. The ideology eats its own. This happens slowly, at scales larger than any individual life, and waiting for it is not a strategy. But it is a reason not to let it take your entire interior life as the cost of paying attention to it.

You cannot change what you cannot reach. You can change what you are doing with the part of reality that is actually yours — your attention, your thinking, your willingness to encounter ideas that challenge rather than confirm, your capacity to find something worth caring about in the immediate and specific rather than the distant and abstract. That is not a small thing. It is, in fact, the only thing that has ever actually changed anything, at the scale of a person. Which is the only scale any individual ever operates at, regardless of what the algorithm would like you to believe.
