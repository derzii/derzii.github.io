Atheists Reject God So They Can Sin?

On the accusation that disbelief is just moral convenience

Philosophy 19 Apr 2026

The argument is older than the internet and simpler than it appears: the reason people reject God is not intellectual. It is moral. They do not want to be accountable to a standard they cannot meet, so they remove the standard. They do not want to feel guilty about what they are doing, so they eliminate the thing that produces the guilt. The disbelief is not a conclusion. It is a permission slip. They rejected God so they could sin without consequence.

This argument is taken seriously by a significant number of people. It is worth taking seriously in return — not because it is correct, but because it contains a real observation that has been extended far past where the observation actually reaches, and understanding where it goes wrong reveals something worth knowing about how accusations of this kind function.

01   What the argument gets right

There is a real phenomenon being pointed at. Some people do adjust their stated beliefs in order to reduce the friction between those beliefs and their behavior. This happens in every domain, not just religion. The person who wants to continue an obviously harmful relationship will develop beliefs that make the relationship seem less harmful. The person who benefits from an unjust arrangement will develop beliefs that make the arrangement seem just. Motivated reasoning — the process of working backward from a desired conclusion to the beliefs that support it — is a genuine feature of human cognition, not an invented one.

Applied to religion specifically: yes, there are people who find the moral demands of their tradition inconvenient, and whose departure from belief is entangled with their desire to escape those demands. This happens. It is probably not the primary driver for most people who leave religion, but it is not an invented motivation. The accusation is not pointing at nothing.

Where it goes catastrophically wrong is in the move from "this happens sometimes" to "this is why atheists do not believe." That move converts a partial observation into a universal explanation — and in doing so, it exempts the accuser from having to engage with the actual reasons people give for not believing, because those reasons have been pre-explained away as rationalization. This is not an argument. It is a way of blocking arguments before they can be made.

02   The structure of the accusation

Notice what the accusation does epistemically. It assigns a motive to the disbeliever that makes their reasoning irrelevant. Whatever argument they give for not believing — the problem of evil, the absence of evidence, the incoherence of certain theological claims, the conflict between scripture and what we know about the physical world — all of it can be dismissed under this framework as rationalization. The real reason is moral convenience. The arguments are just cover.

This is a closed loop. It cannot be falsified, because any counter-evidence the atheist provides can be interpreted as further evidence of the motivated reasoning. The more sophisticated the argument against belief, the more elaborate the rationalization must be, which confirms how badly the person wants to avoid accountability. The accusation is structured to be immune to the very thing that would refute it: the atheist's actual reasoning.

A framework that cannot be challenged by evidence is not a conclusion. It is a defensive posture. And the function of this particular defensive posture is to protect the believer from having to take seriously the arguments for disbelief. If the disbeliever's reasoning is always secretly motivated by the desire to sin, then their reasoning never has to be addressed on its own terms. The intellectual challenge is converted into a moral accusation, which is much easier to respond to because moral accusations do not require engaging with arguments.

03   The empirical problem

If the theory were correct — if people rejected God primarily to avoid moral accountability — you would expect atheists to behave significantly worse than believers by the standards the accusation implies. More dishonesty. More cruelty. More of whatever the relevant tradition defines as sin. The removal of the constraint should produce more of the constrained behavior.

The evidence does not support this. Measures of honesty, generosity, criminal behavior, and general ethical conduct do not show atheists systematically worse than religious believers. Some measures show differences in specific behaviors, as you would expect when different frameworks define different things as important. But the picture of the morally unmoored atheist, freed from constraint and acting accordingly, is not what the data shows. It is what the theory predicts. The theory and the data do not agree.

There is also the straightforward observation that many atheists hold moral commitments that are, by any reasonable standard, as serious and demanding as those held by religious believers — and in some cases more demanding, because they are not outsourced to a divine authority but carried entirely by the individual's own sense of what is right. The atheist who treats honesty as non-negotiable without the support of a divine command is not doing something easier than the believer who treats it as non-negotiable because God requires it. The atheist has no backstop. The standard is held entirely by themselves, which is a harder thing to maintain, not an easier one.

04   Why morality does not require God

The deeper assumption underneath the accusation is that morality requires a divine source. Without God, there is no real moral standard — only preferences, conventions, agreements that can be renegotiated. Therefore, removing God removes the moral framework, and the person who removes God is removing the framework on purpose, because the framework was the inconvenient part.

This is a serious philosophical position and it deserves a serious response. The argument that without God everything is permitted has a real history and real defenders. It is not stupid. But it is also not obviously correct, and the assumption that it settles the question — that without God there is no morality, full stop — is not a demonstrated conclusion. It is a premise held with the confidence of a conclusion.

There are robust philosophical accounts of morality that do not depend on divine command. The observation that suffering is bad, that consistency in treatment matters, that beings capable of having interests deserve to have those interests considered — these do not require a God to be compelling. You can arrive at serious moral commitments from the recognition that other people are real, that their experience matters, and that the same reasoning that makes your own suffering bad makes theirs bad too. This is not a perfect moral theory. No moral theory is perfect. But it is a foundation, and it does not require believing in a God to hold it.

The person who has rejected God and arrived at this kind of secular ethical commitment is not a person who removed the constraint so they could behave badly. They are a person who found the divine-command foundation inadequate and built a different one. Whether the different one is better is a genuine philosophical question. That they built one at all refutes the claim that the rejection was primarily about escaping accountability.

05   What the accusation is really protecting

When an argument is structured to be immune to counter-evidence, the question worth asking is not whether the argument is true. The question is what it is protecting. What does it allow the person making it to avoid?

In this case, what it protects is the believer from having to take seriously the reasons people give for not believing. Because if those reasons are real — if the problem of evil is a genuine difficulty, if the absence of evidence is a genuine problem, if the conflicts between certain religious claims and what we know about the world are genuine conflicts — then belief requires a response to them. It requires engaging with the difficulty honestly, sitting with the challenge, doing the hard work of either answering the objection or revising the belief in light of it.

The accusation of moral convenience makes all of that unnecessary. The objections are not real objections. They are rationalizations. The person raising them is not a serious interlocutor. They are someone who wanted permission to behave badly and built an intellectual structure to provide it. You do not have to engage with them. You have already understood them better than they understand themselves.

This is comfortable. It is also a way of never having to examine the foundations of your own belief seriously, because the challenges to those foundations have been pre-explained as bad faith. The believer who uses this argument is not protecting the truth of their belief. They are protecting themselves from the discomfort of taking the challenge to it seriously. Which is a form of the same motivated reasoning they are accusing the atheist of — just applied in the opposite direction, to maintain belief rather than to abandon it.

06   The honest version of the concern

Strip the accusation down to what is genuinely worth worrying about and something real remains: the concern that without shared moral foundations, shared life becomes harder. That if different people are operating from fundamentally different frameworks — different sources of moral authority, different definitions of what matters — then the project of living together becomes more difficult, more contested, more prone to the kind of conflict that has no resolution because the parties are not even speaking the same moral language.

This is a legitimate concern. It is not the same as the accusation, but it is related to it, and it deserves to be engaged with rather than dismissed. The question of how people with different foundational commitments can share a world, make agreements, and hold each other accountable is a real question. It does not have an easy answer.

What it does not require is the premise that people without religious belief have abandoned moral seriousness. You can take the concern seriously without accepting the caricature. The atheist is not a person who has removed all constraints and is now coasting on the permission that disbelief provides. They are a person operating from a different foundation — one that may be more or less robust than the religious alternative, but that is a philosophical question to be argued, not a psychological diagnosis to be assumed.

The argument that atheists reject God to sin mistakes a motive for a conclusion. It mistakes the desire for permission for the reason for disbelief. And in doing so it closes off exactly the conversation that would be most useful: the honest examination of what grounds morality, what it requires, and how people with different answers to that question can nevertheless hold each other to standards that make shared life possible.

That conversation requires treating the other person as someone who has reasons, not as someone who has desires dressed up as reasons. It requires the basic intellectual courtesy of engaging with what people actually say rather than explaining it away before it can be heard. Most people are not willing to extend that courtesy across a disagreement this fundamental. Which is why the accusation keeps circulating, and why the conversation it forecloses keeps not happening.
