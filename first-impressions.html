The Thing About First Impressions

On snapshots we defend for years

Observation Mar 2025

You meet someone once, in a bad moment — they're distracted, or tired, or just having a day that doesn't represent them. And that's who they become. Not the version that exists across hundreds of hours, but the snapshot from a Tuesday afternoon when something had gone wrong. You carry it for years, sometimes forever, and call it knowing them.

First impressions are efficient. That's the honest thing to say about them. The brain needs shortcuts, and the first reading of a person is the most available one. What's strange is not that we form them — it's how tenaciously we defend them. New information gets filtered. Contradictory evidence gets explained away. The snapshot becomes load-bearing.

We don't update our impressions of people the way we update facts. We update facts reluctantly. We update people almost never.

Part of this is about the cost of being wrong. To revise a first impression requires admitting that the original was a mistake — that you misread someone, filed them incorrectly, perhaps even treated them according to a version of themselves that didn't exist. That's uncomfortable. It's easier to hold the original read and interpret the contradictions as exceptions.

The more interesting question is what first impressions are really measuring. Confidence, certainly. Warmth. The legibility of someone's social signals. But these are surface things — the performance a person gives before they trust you, before they're tired, before the context stops being formal. What we're actually reading, most of the time, is how well someone manages a first encounter. Which is a skill. Not a character.

Some of the most compelling people are terrible at first impressions. They're too honest too early, or too quiet in the wrong company, or they simply don't perform the rituals of likeability with enough fluency. And some of the smoothest first impressions conceal very little of interest behind them.

The snapshot is not the film. Most people are worth the longer viewing.
