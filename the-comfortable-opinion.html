The Comfortable Opinion

Philosophy May 2025

Most people, if pressed, could not tell you when they formed their most important beliefs. Not really. The beliefs are just there — arrived at through some combination of upbringing, environment, and the path of least resistance. They feel like conclusions. They are rarely the result of inquiry.

This is not a criticism. Forming original opinions from scratch on every question would be paralyzing. We inherit most of what we believe, and that is largely fine. The problem is when we mistake inheritance for reasoning — when we defend an idea as if we thought it through, when what we actually did was receive it without resistance.

The comfortable opinion isn't the one you agree with. It's the one you've never had to fight for, and so never had to truly examine.

What makes an opinion comfortable is not that it's pleasant — some people hold bleak views quite contentedly — but that it has never been seriously threatened. The comfortable opinion is the one that has lived entirely among friends. It has never had to explain itself to someone who genuinely doesn't share its assumptions.

There is a kind of intellectual courage that looks like stubbornness from the outside: the willingness to sit with a challenge to something you believe, without immediately reaching for a counter-argument. Just holding the discomfort. Letting the other view actually land before you start dismantling it.

Most people skip this step entirely. The counter-argument is already loading before the challenge is finished. Which means they are not engaging with the idea — they are defending the position. And defending a position is very different from thinking about whether it deserves to be defended.

The uncomfortable question is not "am I right?" Most people ask that and answer yes immediately. The uncomfortable question is "what would have to be true for me to be wrong?" And then sitting with that, genuinely, for longer than feels necessary.
